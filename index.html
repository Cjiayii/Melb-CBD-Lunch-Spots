Five Japanese Lunch Spots in Melbourne CBD That Office Workers Will Actually Return To

Some lunches are just functional. These are not.

Fast enough for weekdays, good enough for weekends.
Best for solo lunch Under A$20 Classic ramen Quick sushi

If your workday lives somewhere between a calendar full of meetings and a half-hour break that disappears too quickly, the best lunch spots do three things well: they’re reliable, they’re fast enough for the CBD rhythm, and they leave you feeling like you ate something worth stepping out for. The five places below are my current editorial top picks for Japanese lunch in Melbourne CBD, ranked for a mix of city-worker practicality, strong public review coverage, and value cues in current public sources.

The result is a list that leans warm, comforting, and quietly addictive: glossy udon, deeply savoury ramen, sushi that moves fast, and one very good omurice for the days you want something a little different.

The Five

01

Yamamoto Udon

Estimated spend: A$19–25 Location: Melbourne CBD Best for solo lunch

There’s something especially appealing about a lunch spot that feels calm without being slow. Yamamoto Udon was recently reviewed by Time Out as a highly focused CBD udon specialist, with food noted to arrive quickly and a room designed for solo diners and efficient weekday eating. The standout dishes in that review were the Wagyu mapo tofu udon ($19) and the beef udon ($19) with tempura, both described as carefully presented and deeply satisfying.

What to order first

Start with the Wagyu mapo tofu udon if you want something richer and more distinctive, or the beef udon if you’re after a cleaner, restorative bowl with a softer, more classic feel.

Why it works for office workers

It reads like the rare lunch place that feels polished, efficient, and self-contained — exactly the kind of spot you can slip into alone and leave feeling reset.

02

Hakata Gensuke

Estimated spend: up to A$20 168 Russell Street Classic ramen

Some lunches are about comfort; this one is about conviction. Time Out’s ramen guide still places Hakata Gensuke at the top of Melbourne’s ramen conversation, especially for its tonkotsu broth, and its older Russell Street review describes a fully customisable bowl with broth, noodle firmness, and toppings all adjusted to taste. The same review says to prepare to pay up to $20 for a fully dressed bowl, and calls out the venue’s Time Out Food Award for Best Bang for Buck.

What to order first

The most commonly recommended moves are the black tonkotsu for its garlic-and-black-sesame edge or the God Fire ramen if you like heat. If you have room, the review also singles out the pork and cabbage gyoza and karaage as smart add-ons.

Hours

Mon–Fri 11:30am–10pm; Sat–Sun noon–10pm.

This is the lunch you choose when you want a bowl that feels almost ceremonial in its seriousness — rich, warm, and worth queueing for.

Source noted in article: timeout.com
03

Mr Ramen San

Estimated spend: under A$20 MidCity Centre / Bourke Street arcade precinct Repeat weekday habit

If you want the dependable city-core lunch that keeps regulars coming back, Mr Ramen San is still one of the strongest cases. Time Out’s 2025 ramen roundup explicitly calls it a cheap CBD lunch under $20, and its longer review praises the shop’s softer, less heavy tonkotsu broth, house-made noodles, and the fact that the bowls satisfy without flattening the rest of your afternoon. Time Out also notes that the venue sits in MidCity Centre / MidCity Arcade and has a strong vegan and vegetarian offering.

What to order first

Go classic with the tonkotsu ramen if it’s your first visit, but the critic’s real left-field favourite was the mazesoba, a rich, soupy-less noodle dish layered with garlic, chicken, roast pork, bonito, chives and chilli oil.

The whole appeal here is that it feels like a proper weekday habit: unfussy, filling, and easy to justify more than once a week.

Source noted in article: timeout.com
04

Tokyo Motto

Estimated spend: A$12.50–20 Shop 23 & 23A, Midcity Centre, 200 Bourke Street Most playful pick

For the days when ramen feels too obvious and a sandwich feels too forgettable, Tokyo Motto is the playful alternative. Time Out’s feature on the venue says the shop’s tornado omurice is $12.50, describes that dish as a key lure, and also highlights the venue’s signature Japanese curry, spicy miso ramen, and katsu.

What to order first

If it’s your first visit, it should probably be the omurice. After that, the Golden curry and spicy miso ramen are the next logical moves.

Hours

Mon–Thu noon–11pm; Fri–Sun noon–1am.

What I like most about this pick is that it gives the list some colour. It still works as lunch, but it has a little nightlife energy in the bones — which makes it especially good for the kind of midday break that needs rescuing.

Source noted in article: timeout.com
05

Sushi Hotaru

Estimated spend: A$13–20 Mid City Centre Level 1, Shop 118, 200 Bourke Street Quick sushi

Not every office lunch needs to be steaming and soupy. Some days you want speed, variety, and a spend that stays polite. Time Out describes Sushi Hotaru as a well-known budget sushi option in Mid City Centre, with efficient service, sushi that is ready to eat, and plates priced from $3.30 up to $8.50 for deluxe dishes. It also says the average wait is about 20 minutes and lists the venue as open daily from 11:30am to 10pm.

What to order first

Time Out highlights the soft-shell crab, seared wagyu beef, and grilled eel with cream cheese sushi as the plates to know.

This is the lunch for people who want optionality: a few plates and back to the office, or a bigger stack if the afternoon looks brutal.

The best work lunches are rarely the flashiest ones. They’re the places that fit into real life.

The Soft Conclusion

Somewhere warm when Melbourne turns cold, somewhere quick when your calendar is unreasonable, somewhere good enough to make a Tuesday feel less like a Tuesday.

If I were shaping this list purely for repeat weekday use, I’d keep coming back to Yamamoto Udon for polish, Mr Ramen San for comfort, and Tokyo Motto for a lunch that feels a little more fun than it needs to be.
